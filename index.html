❤️ Atrial Fibrillation → Stroke Mechanism

Step-by-step: Left atrium fibrillates (quivers) → blood pools/stagnates → clot forms in left atrium → clot travels: left atrium → left ventricle → aorta → common carotid → internal carotid → Middle Cerebral Artery → ISCHEMIC STROKE

This is why Afib patients need anticoagulation (warfarin, NOACs) — to prevent clot formation in the left atrium.

⚡ Myelin: Why It Matters

Myelin is the fatty insulating sheath wrapped around axons. It enables saltatory conduction — the signal "jumps" from Node of Ranvier to Node of Ranvier at up to 120 m/s. Without myelin, signals travel slowly (~1 m/s) or stop entirely. In the CNS (brain/spinal cord), myelin is made by oligodendrocytes. In the PNS (peripheral nerves), by Schwann cells.

🔑 Exam Distinction:
MS → oligodendrocytes (CNS) destroyed → brain/spinal cord plaques
GBS → Schwann cells (PNS) attacked → ascending peripheral weakness

⚡ Seizures: Neurons Gone Haywire

Neurons communicate via controlled electrical impulses — action potentials. A seizure happens when a group of neurons fires chaotically and synchronously at the same time, like a power surge. This abnormal electrical burst can stay localized (focal seizure) or spread across both hemispheres (generalized). Anti-seizure drugs like phenytoin block voltage-gated sodium channels — they raise the threshold for firing, making it harder for neurons to sustain the rapid, repetitive firing of a seizure.

🔑 SAND = Phenytoin toxicity: Sedation · Ataxia · Nystagmus · Diplopia

🔌 Types of Neurons

Motor neurons
Carry commands FROM brain/spinal cord TO muscles. Damaged in ALS (upper & lower motor neurons) and GBS (peripheral motor fibers).

Carry signals FROM body TO brain. Damaged in GBS (ascending sensory loss starts in feet), SCI (loss below injury level).

Connect neurons within the CNS. Most numerous. Involved in reflexes and seizure propagation.

⚡ Action Potential — How Neurons Fire

Resting
−70 mV inside cell. Na⁺ outside, K⁺ inside. Stable.
📶

Stimulus opens Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ rushes IN → inside becomes positive.
🔺

Na⁺ channels close. K⁺ channels open → K⁺ flows OUT.
📉

Cell returns to negative inside. Brief hyperpolarization (refractory period).

Na⁺/K⁺ pump restores balance. Ready to fire again.
💊 How drugs work here: Phenytoin & Levetiracetam block Na⁺ channels → harder to sustain rapid firing → seizure threshold raised. Mannitol does NOT affect action potentials — it works osmotically to pull fluid from brain cells.

🔌 The Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ) & Synapse

The NMJ is where motor neurons talk to muscles using acetylcholine (ACh). Click any labeled part to understand what it does — and how Myasthenia Gravis, pyridostigmine, and other drugs affect it.

⚠️ Myasthenia Gravis (MG) at the NMJ

1.Autoantibodies attack ACh receptors on the muscle fiber
2.Fewer receptors available → ACh has less to bind to
3.Repeated nerve firing depletes available ACh → weakness worsens with activity
4.Rest allows ACh to replenish → improves with rest
💊 Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) — blocks AChE (the enzyme that breaks down ACh) → more ACh remains in the cleft → more binding → temporary strength improvement

✅ Normal NMJ Sequence

1.Action potential arrives at axon terminal
2.Ca²⁺ enters terminal → triggers ACh vesicle fusion
3.ACh released into synaptic cleft
4.ACh binds to nicotinic receptors → Na⁺ enters muscle → depolarization
5.Muscle contracts
6.AChE breaks down ACh → cleft cleared → ready to fire again

🔗 GBS at the Peripheral Nerve

While GBS doesn't affect the NMJ itself, it damages the peripheral nerve axons and myelin that carry signals TO the NMJ. Without the nerve signal arriving, the NMJ has nothing to transmit — the muscle can't contract even if the NMJ is intact.

GBS pattern: Signal never reaches NMJ (axon/myelin destroyed) → ascending weakness feet→legs→trunk→diaphragm.

MG pattern: Signal arrives at NMJ fine, but receptors are destroyed → weakness worsens with repeated use, improves with rest.

🏙️ Think of the brain as a city, and blood vessels as roads

Every neighborhood (brain region) needs constant delivery of oxygen and glucose — 24/7, no breaks. A stroke is when one road (blood vessel) either gets blocked (ischemic = traffic jam) or breaks (hemorrhagic = burst pipe). The neighborhood it supplies loses its supply and starts shutting down. Which neighborhood loses power = which symptoms the patient shows. That's why knowing brain anatomy is key!

🔀 What is this?
Decussation — Why the Brain Controls the Opposite Side

Decussation is the crossing of nerve tracts from one side of the brain to the other, occurring at the medulla oblongata (base of the brain). About 80% of the motor fibers in the corticospinal tract (the main movement highway) cross here. This is why a stroke on the left side of the brain causes weakness on the right side of the body — and vice versa. Think of it like an X-shaped highway interchange.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Every time you assess a stroke patient, you use decussation. If a patient has right-sided weakness → you know the LEFT hemisphere is affected → which also contains the language centers → watch for aphasia. Location of deficit = location of brain damage.

🔀 Decussation — Most Tested Concept!

Rule: ~80% of corticospinal nerves CROSS (decussate) at the medulla oblongata. Each brain hemisphere controls the opposite (contralateral) side of the body. 20% remain ipsilateral.

⚡ What is this?
Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident / CVA)

A stroke happens when blood supply to part of the brain is suddenly cut off. Brain cells need oxygen and glucose every second — without blood flow, they begin dying in minutes. There are two main types: Ischemic stroke (87% of cases) — a clot blocks a blood vessel, like a traffic jam stopping deliveries to a neighborhood. Hemorrhagic stroke (13%) — a blood vessel bursts, like a pipe breaking and flooding the area. Which abilities are lost depends entirely on which part of the brain loses its blood supply.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Stroke is a time-critical emergency. Every minute = ~2 million neurons lost. Your job is to recognize it fast (BEFAST), call the stroke team, and act without hesitation. Treatment decisions (tPA, thrombectomy) depend on type, timing, and contraindications — all things you assess.
⚡ Stroke — Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic at a Glance
Always confirm stroke type with CT scan BEFORE any treatment. Giving tPA to a hemorrhagic stroke patient is fatal.
🚨 FAST — Recognizing a Stroke
FAST is the most important stroke recognition tool. Note the EXACT time symptoms started — this determines tPA eligibility.

💉 What is this?
tPA — Tissue Plasminogen Activator (Alteplase)

tPA is the 'clot-busting' drug used in ischemic stroke. It works by activating plasminogen, a protein already in the blood, which converts to plasmin — essentially chemical scissors that cut apart the fibrin threads holding the clot together. The clot dissolves, blood flow restores, and the penumbra (the still-alive brain tissue surrounding the dead core) is saved. Critical window: within 4 hours of symptom onset (3 hours for some patients). After that, bleeding risk outweighs benefit.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: You are the tPA safety checkpoint. You verify: CT head done (no hemorrhage), last-known-well time, BP <185/110, glucose 50–400, no contraindications. During infusion: neuro checks every 15 min. Any sudden headache or LOC change = stop infusion, call MD — this means intracranial hemorrhage.
⏱️ tPA Time Window — Every Minute Counts
Document the exact "last-known-well" time immediately. If exact time unknown, it is the last time the patient was observed as normal.

🔵 Ischemic Stroke
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
A clot (thrombus or travelling embolus) blocks a cerebral artery. The brain tissue downstream is starved of oxygen and glucose. The core (centre of blocked area) dies within minutes. The surrounding penumbra is in danger but still alive — this is what you are racing to save. Every minute = 1.9 million neurons lost.
👁 What would I see?
  • BEFAST: Balance↓, Eyes blurred, Face drooping (ask to smile), Arm weak (hold both arms up), Speech slurred or absent, Time = call 911
  • Contralateral weakness — LEFT hemisphere stroke = RIGHT arm/leg weak + aphasia
  • Expressive aphasia (Broca's): knows what to say, cannot get words out
  • Receptive aphasia (Wernicke's): speaks fluently but words are jumbled/nonsensical
  • Neglect (right hemisphere): ignores affected side
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • Airway, breathing, circulation first
  • Get CT without contrast IMMEDIATELY — must rule out hemorrhage before ANY treatment
  • Note exact time of symptom onset (or last-known-well time)
  • Call stroke team / activate stroke protocol
  • Check blood glucose (hypoglycaemia mimics stroke)
  • Do NOT give aspirin or tPA until CT confirms ischemic (not hemorrhagic)
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Give tPA right away" — NO. CT without contrast must come FIRST
  • "tPA window is 4.5 hours" — Standard eligibility is 4 hours for most patients
  • "Left hemisphere stroke causes LEFT-sided weakness" — NO. Decussation = LEFT brain controls RIGHT body
  • "Patient had a headache so it must be hemorrhagic" — headache alone does not diagnose type; CT is required
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Any new or worsening neurological deficits after tPA (possible hemorrhagic conversion)
  • BP >185/110 mmHg (affects tPA eligibility; must be controlled before giving)
  • Blood glucose <60 or >400
  • Sudden severe headache after tPA (intracranial bleed — stop infusion immediately)
  • Signs of Cushing's triad (late ↑ICP sign)
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
Position: HOB 0-15° initially to maximise cerebral perfusion (then reassess). NPO until swallow screen passed — aspiration is a major post-stroke risk. Keep room quiet and calm. One instruction at a time for aphasia patients — be patient, don't speak loudly (they're not deaf).
🔴 Hemorrhagic Stroke
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
A cerebral blood vessel ruptures. Blood floods into brain tissue (intracerebral) or the subarachnoid space (SAH). The blood itself is toxic to neurons. The accumulating blood raises ICP, compressing surrounding tissue. SAH commonly caused by ruptured aneurysm.
👁 What would I see?
  • SAH hallmark: sudden, explosive "thunderclap" headache — worst headache of their life
  • Rapid decreased LOC, vomiting (often projectile, no nausea), neck stiffness (meningismus from SAH)
  • Focal deficits from intracerebral bleed (location-dependent)
  • Photophobia, altered pupils
  • Signs of rising ICP: decreasing GCS, Cushing's triad (late)
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • ABSOLUTELY NO tPA, aspirin, heparin, or anticoagulants — fatal if given
  • Manage BP carefully — lower it but maintain adequate CPP (>60 mmHg)
  • For SAH: administer nimodipine (PO/NG only) to prevent vasospasm
  • Seizure precautions
  • Neuro checks every 1-2 hours; ICP monitoring if ordered
  • Keep environment quiet and dim (reduces stimulation-driven ICP spikes)
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "It's a stroke so give tPA" — NEVER. This would be fatal in hemorrhagic stroke
  • "Lower BP aggressively to normal" — No, too-rapid BP drop reduces CPP → brain ischemia
  • "Nimodipine can be given IV" — NO. Nimodipine = PO/NG tube ONLY; IV causes fatal hypotension
  • "Headache = tension headache" — Thunderclap headache is SAH until proven otherwise
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Cushing's triad (call MD immediately — brainstem herniation is occurring)
  • New or worsening focal deficits
  • Declining GCS score (even 1-2 points)
  • Seizure activity
  • Vasospasm signs in SAH (delayed neuro deterioration days 4–14)
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
"When in doubt, it is hemorrhagic until CT proves otherwise." No clot-busting agents, no blood thinners. Give nimodipine for SAH — set a phone alarm to never miss a dose. Vasospasm peaks days 4-14 post-SAH, so watch closely beyond admission day.
⚠️ TIA — Transient Ischemic Attack
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Temporary blockage of a cerebral artery — same mechanism as ischemic stroke, but the clot dissolves or dislodges on its own. Symptoms fully resolve within 24 hours with NO permanent damage. However, TIA is a serious warning: up to 15% of patients have a full stroke within 3 months if untreated.
👁 What would I see?
  • Identical to ischemic stroke symptoms — but they resolve
  • Face droop, arm weakness, speech problems — gone within minutes to hours
  • RIND: symptoms last >24h but <weeks and then fully resolve
  • Patient may downplay it because "it went away"
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • Never dismiss because symptoms resolved — treat urgently
  • Full stroke workup: CT, MRI, carotid Doppler, cardiac monitoring (Afib?), lipids, glucose
  • Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin/clopidogrel) typically started
  • Risk factor education: BP, cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, Afib
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "TIA resolved, patient can go home" — No. Must be evaluated and monitored for stroke risk
  • "TIA is a minor stroke" — technically it causes no permanent damage, but risk of true stroke is very high soon after
  • Confusing RIND with CVA — RIND resolves (like TIA); CVA does not fully resolve
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Any return of neurological symptoms — call 911 immediately
  • New onset symptoms after discharge
  • Heart rhythm abnormalities (Afib = high embolic risk)
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
Teach the patient: if any symptoms return, even briefly, call 911 immediately — do not wait for them to go away again. A TIA is the brain sending a warning siren. A nurse who dismisses it is missing a chance to prevent a stroke.

🔌 Think of neurons as appliances in a house

Each neuron needs wiring (myelin) to conduct signals, an outlet (neurotransmitter receptor) to receive signals, and a power source (neurotransmitter supply). Neurodegenerative diseases break one of these: MS/GBS strip the wire insulation (demyelination) → signals short out. Parkinson's drains the power supply (dopamine) → appliances run slowly or not at all. MG blocks the outlet (ACh receptors) → muscles can't receive the signal. Alzheimer's destroys the appliance itself (neurons) → function is lost permanently.

⚡ What is this?
Seizures

A seizure is an episode of abnormal, uncontrolled electrical activity in the brain. Normally, neurons fire in organized patterns. In a seizure, a group of neurons 'short-circuits' and fires chaotically all at once — like a power surge overwhelming a circuit. Depending on where in the brain this happens and how far it spreads, the patient may experience muscle jerking, staring spells, altered consciousness, strange sensations, or automatisms (repetitive movements like lip-smacking). Seizures are symptoms — they always have an underlying cause (epilepsy, brain injury, low blood sugar, alcohol withdrawal, head trauma, infection, electrolyte imbalance).

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: During a seizure: protect (don't restrain, pad rails, turn to side after), time it (anything >5 min = status epilepticus = call MD stat), document exactly what you see. After: assess LOC, injury, postictal state. A patient who is confused after a 'fainting episode' is actually postictal — crucial distinction from a simple faint.

🤯 What is this?
Headaches — Primary vs. Secondary

A headache is pain anywhere in the head or upper neck. From a nursing standpoint, the critical distinction is: Primary headaches (the headache IS the condition — migraine, tension, cluster) vs. Secondary headaches (the headache is a SYMPTOM of something dangerous — meningitis, stroke, ↑ICP, subarachnoid hemorrhage, hypertensive emergency). 🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Your job: distinguish dangerous from benign. Red flags = 'SNOOP': Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss), Neurological symptoms, Onset sudden, Older age (first severe HA >50), Previous headache change. Any of these → escalate immediately.

🤲 What is this?
Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused by the slow death of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra (a region in the brainstem). Dopamine is the neurotransmitter that fine-tunes movement — it's what makes movement smooth, fast, and coordinated. As dopamine levels fall, the brain loses its 'movement regulator.' The result: tremor at rest, muscle rigidity (stiffness), bradykinesia (extreme slowness), and postural instability. It is NOT just shaking — it's a whole-body movement disorder. There is no cure; treatment aims to replace or mimic dopamine to manage symptoms.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Parkinson's patients are at extreme fall risk and aspiration risk. Your priorities: give Sinemet (levodopa/carbidopa) EXACTLY on time — even 30 minutes late can leave a patient unable to swallow or walk. Assess swallowing before every meal. Never hold the medication to 'reduce fall risk' — it makes everything worse.

💪 What is this?
ALS — Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (Lou Gehrig's Disease)

Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis is a rapidly progressive, fatal disease that destroys both upper motor neurons (in the brain and spinal cord) and lower motor neurons (that directly connect to muscles). The result: muscles progressively waste and weaken — starting anywhere (hand, foot, speech) and spreading to all voluntary muscles. Eventually the diaphragm is affected → respiratory failure, which is the cause of death. Crucially, cognition and sensation are typically preserved — the patient is fully aware of their deterioration. Average survival is 2–5 years from diagnosis.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: ALS care is about maintaining quality of life and preparing for respiratory decline. Monitor respiratory function closely (FVC% — forced vital capacity). The decision about mechanical ventilation is deeply personal. Advance directives and a palliative care team are essential. Nutritional support (often via PEG tube) and communication devices become critical as the disease progresses.

🔗 What is this?
MS — Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple Sclerosis is an autoimmune disease in which the body's immune system attacks and destroys the myelin sheath — the fatty insulation around nerve fibers in the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord). Without myelin, nerve signals slow down, become distorted, or stop entirely — like a frayed electrical wire. Scar tissue (sclerosis) forms at the attack sites (plaques), especially in periventricular white matter. Symptoms vary wildly depending on which nerve pathways are affected: vision problems (optic neuritis), weakness, spasticity, fatigue, bladder dysfunction, balance problems. Most common form: relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS) — periods of attacks (relapses) followed by partial or complete recovery (remission).

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: MS management focuses on: disease-modifying therapy (to slow progression), managing relapses (often IV steroids), and symptom control. Key nursing points: heat worsens MS symptoms (Uhthoff's phenomenon) — avoid baths >98°F; fatigue is neurological, not laziness; bladder dysfunction increases UTI risk; depression is common. Never give live vaccines during immunosuppressive therapy.

📈 What is this?
GBS — Guillain-Barré Syndrome

Guillain-Barré Syndrome is an acute autoimmune attack on the myelin sheath of the peripheral nervous system — the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord. It almost always follows an infection (GI or respiratory, most commonly Campylobacter jejuni) by 2–4 weeks. The immune system makes antibodies to fight the infection but accidentally attacks peripheral nerve myelin too. The defining feature: ascending weakness — starting in the feet and legs and rising toward the trunk, arms, and eventually the diaphragm. Without the diaphragm, the patient cannot breathe → respiratory failure is the life-threatening emergency. Most patients (80%+) recover with treatment, but it takes months.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: GBS nursing is about respiratory surveillance. Monitor vital capacity (VC) frequently — if VC falls below 20 mL/kg or 1 liter, prepare for intubation. Watch for autonomic instability (wild BP and HR swings, arrhythmias). Strict NPO if bulbar symptoms. Emotional support is critical — the patient is fully awake and aware while becoming paralyzed.

👁 What is this?
MG — Myasthenia Gravis

Myasthenia Gravis is an autoimmune disease in which the body produces antibodies that attack and destroy acetylcholine (ACh) receptors at the neuromuscular junction — the gap where nerves connect to muscles. Without enough receptors, the muscle can't receive the nerve's signal to contract. The hallmark: muscle weakness that worsens with use and improves with rest. Classic early sign: ptosis (drooping eyelid) and diplopia (double vision) that worsen throughout the day. Bulbar symptoms (difficulty swallowing, nasal voice) signal danger. Myasthenic crisis = severe weakness leading to respiratory failure. Cholinergic crisis = too much ACh medication causing SLUDGE symptoms + weakness.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Distinguishing myasthenic crisis from cholinergic crisis is a high-stakes nursing skill. Both cause weakness and breathing difficulty. The difference: SLUDGE symptoms (Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation/Diarrhea, GI cramps, Emesis) = cholinergic crisis → HOLD pyridostigmine + give atropine. No SLUDGE = myasthenic crisis → may need more medication or IVIG/plasmapheresis. When in doubt → call the physician.

🕺 What is this?
Huntington's Disease

Huntington's disease is a hereditary, progressive neurodegenerative disorder caused by a mutation in the HTT gene (chromosome 4) that leads to the gradual death of neurons, particularly in the basal ganglia and cerebral cortex. It causes a devastating triad: motor symptoms (chorea — involuntary, jerky, dance-like movements), cognitive decline (dementia), and psychiatric symptoms (depression, anxiety, personality changes). Onset is typically 30–50 years old. Because it is autosomal dominant, each child of an affected parent has a 50% chance of inheriting it. There is no cure — death typically occurs 15–20 years after diagnosis.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Huntington's nursing focuses on safety (fall prevention with chorea), nutrition (high-calorie diet as the body burns energy with constant involuntary movements), aspiration precautions, and emotional/family support. The genetic counseling aspect is profound — family members may be deciding whether to get tested for the gene. Depression and suicide risk are high. A multidisciplinary team including neurologist, speech therapy, OT/PT, and social work is essential.

⚡ Seizures
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Abnormal, excessive synchronised electrical discharge in neurons — an "electrical storm" in the brain. Can be generalised (whole brain) or focal (one area). Cause: lowered seizure threshold from epilepsy, brain injury, fever, hypoglycaemia, electrolyte imbalance, or medication non-compliance.
👁 What would I see?
  • Tonic-clonic: sudden stiffening (tonic) → rhythmic jerking (clonic), eyes may deviate, cyanosis
  • Absence: blank stare, eye blinking, no response for seconds — easily missed
  • Focal aware: one limb jerking, patient conscious
  • Postictal phase: confusion, fatigue, headache, weakness (Todd's paralysis) — normal after grand mal
  • Aura: strange smell, déjà vu, flashing lights BEFORE seizure — patient-specific warning
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • During seizure: protect from injury — pad rails, remove objects, do NOT restrain
  • Turn on LEFT side (recovery position) to prevent aspiration
  • Time the seizure from start — if >5 minutes = status epilepticus = call MD now
  • Do NOT put anything in the patient's mouth
  • After: assess LOC, check for injury, document everything
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Put a bite block or spoon in the mouth" — NEVER. Causes fractures, injuries, aspiration
  • "Restrain the patient during the seizure" — No. Restraining causes injuries
  • "Patient can't swallow their tongue" — True, but airway can still be blocked; side position prevents this
  • Confusing postictal confusion with a new stroke — postictal confusion clears, stroke deficits persist
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Seizure >5 minutes (status epilepticus — emergency, give IV benzodiazepine)
  • First-time seizure in any patient
  • Seizure WITH fever (meningitis/encephalitis?)
  • Seizure occurring AFTER tPA in stroke patient (intracranial bleed)
  • Any change in seizure pattern from baseline
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
Never give oral medications during or immediately after a seizure — aspiration risk. Document: time started, time ended, type of movements, loss of consciousness, postictal behaviour. On clinical: check phenytoin levels if patient on Dilantin and has a breakthrough seizure.
🤲 Parkinson's Disease
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Progressive death of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra. Dopamine normally balances ACh for smooth movement. When dopamine drops, ACh activity is unchecked → movement becomes slow, rigid, trembling. The disease is progressive and has no cure.
👁 What would I see?
  • TRAP: Tremor (resting, "pill-rolling" — stops with intentional movement), Rigidity (cogwheel), Akinesia/Bradykinesia (slow movement, mask-like face, soft voice), Postural instability (shuffling gait, festination, falls)
  • Micrographia (tiny handwriting)
  • Drooling (dysphagia)
  • Depression (very common — dopamine affects mood too)
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • Give Sinemet on EXACT schedule — never hold, never delay without MD order. Missing a dose = patient cannot move, speak, or swallow safely
  • Fall precautions (postural instability = high fall risk)
  • Swallowing assessment — aspiration pneumonia is a leading cause of death in Parkinson's
  • Thicken liquids if dysphagia present
  • Allow extra time for ADLs — do not rush the patient
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Parkinson's tremor occurs with movement" — No. Resting tremor = Parkinson's. Action tremor = essential tremor (different condition)
  • "Holding Sinemet to save it for later is fine" — Never. Hospital-acquired immobility from missed Parkinson's meds is a patient safety issue
  • Thinking dopamine can be given directly — dopamine cannot cross the blood-brain barrier; levodopa can
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Inability to swallow (aspiration risk — may need feeding tube)
  • Signs of dyskinesia (involuntary movements = too much L-dopa — tell MD)
  • Sudden inability to move (likely missed dose)
  • Hallucinations/confusion (dopaminergic drug side effect)
  • Fever + rigidity + altered LOC after stopping/holding meds (dopamine agonist withdrawal syndrome)
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
On clinical: when you admit a Parkinson's patient, the FIRST thing you do is reconcile their home medication times and recreate that exact schedule in the MAR. Even 30-60 minutes off schedule matters. Remind all team members not to hold these meds.
🔗 Multiple Sclerosis
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Autoimmune attack on CNS myelin (oligodendrocytes). Demyelinated axons conduct signals slowly or not at all. Repeated attacks form plaques (scars) throughout the brain and spinal cord. Most common form = relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS): episodes of symptoms followed by partial or full recovery.
👁 What would I see?
  • Optic neuritis: blurred/lost vision, pain with eye movement (often first symptom)
  • Lhermitte's sign: electric shock sensation down spine when flexing neck
  • Uhthoff's phenomenon: symptoms worsen with heat (bath, fever, hot weather)
  • Fatigue (most common complaint overall)
  • Spasticity, bladder dysfunction (urgency, retention), cognitive changes, depression
  • Charcot's triad of MS: nystagmus, intention tremor, scanning speech
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • Avoid heat — cooling vests, cool environment, ice packs (Uhthoff's)
  • Fall precautions (weakness, spasticity, fatigue)
  • Bladder management — scheduled voiding, straight catheterisation if retention
  • Disease-modifying therapy (interferons, natalizumab) — monitor injection sites, flu-like side effects
  • Energy conservation strategies (fatigue is real and debilitating)
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Heat helps relax MS muscles" — No. Heat worsens symptoms (Uhthoff's) — use cooling, not warmth
  • "MS always progresses linearly" — No. RRMS patients have episodes with recovery between them
  • "MS symptoms mean permanent damage" — Not necessarily during a relapse — symptoms may fully resolve
📞 What would I report to the provider?

👁 Myasthenia Gravis (MG)
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Autoimmune antibodies destroy ACh receptors at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ). Fewer receptors = weaker muscle response to nerve signals. Classic pattern: weakness WORSENS with activity (muscle receptors become depleted), IMPROVES with rest (receptors partially recover). No cure but manageable.
👁 What would I see?

  • Diplopia (double vision)
  • Dysarthria (slurred speech) and dysphagia (swallowing difficulty)

📦 Think of the skull as a sealed lunchbox

The skull is a rigid, sealed box containing three things: brain tissue, blood, and CSF (Monroe-Kellie doctrine). There's no room for anything extra. If a bleed, swelling, or tumor adds volume, something else has to shrink — or the pressure inside rises dangerously (↑ICP). Too much pressure = the brain gets squeezed downward through small openings (herniation) = fatal. Every ICP intervention is about reducing the stuff inside the box.

📈 What is this?
ICP — Intracranial Pressure & the Monro-Kellie Doctrine

Intracranial Pressure (ICP) is the pressure inside the skull. Normal = 5–15 mmHg. The skull is a rigid, non-expandable box containing three things: brain tissue (80%), blood (10%), and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) (10%). This is the Monro-Kellie Doctrine — if any one component increases in volume, one or both of the others MUST decrease to keep pressure normal. When compensation fails (all three are at max), pressure rises dangerously. High ICP compresses brain structures, cuts off blood flow (reduces CPP = Cerebral Perfusion Pressure), and can cause herniation — the brain being squeezed through openings in the skull — which is fatal.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: You are the first to detect rising ICP. Know the signs in order of progression: early (subtle behavior change, headache, nausea), middle (worsening confusion, pupil changes), late (Cushing's Triad: ↑BP + ↓HR + irregular respirations = herniation is happening → call MD immediately). Position HOB 30°, head midline, avoid anything that raises pressure (coughing, Valsalva, hip flexion >90°).
📈 Monro-Kellie Doctrine — Why ICP Rises
The skull cannot expand. When brain volume increases (edema, bleed, tumor), CSF and blood are displaced first. Once compensation is exhausted, ICP rises sharply. Goal: keep ICP <20 mmHg, CPP >60 mmHg.

🤕 What is this?
TBI — Traumatic Brain Injury

Traumatic Brain Injury occurs when an external force (blow, jolt, penetrating object) disrupts normal brain function. TBI ranges from mild (concussion — brief confusion, no structural damage) to severe (prolonged unconsciousness, structural damage, coma). Two types of brain damage occur: Primary injury — the direct damage at the moment of impact (contusion, laceration, axonal shearing). Secondary injury — the cascade of damage that follows over hours to days: cerebral edema, ↑ICP, hypoxia, hypotension, seizures, infection. The primary injury is already done — nursing focuses almost entirely on preventing secondary injury.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Your secondary injury prevention bundle: maintain SpO₂ ≥94% (hypoxia kills neurons), keep MAP adequate for CPP ≥60, prevent fever (raises metabolic demand), control ICP, prevent seizures, minimize stimulation. The GCS is your monitoring tool — any downward trend is an emergency. Know your baseline and reassess frequently.

🩸 What is this?
Intracranial Hematomas — Blood Where It Shouldn't Be

An intracranial hematoma is a collection of blood inside the skull, compressing the brain. Three main locations: Epidural hematoma — blood between skull and dura (usually from middle meningeal artery rupture after temporal skull fracture). Classic: brief LOC → lucid interval → rapid deterioration. Subdural hematoma — blood between dura and arachnoid (from torn bridging veins, common in elderly/anticoagulated). Can be acute, subacute, or chronic. Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) — blood in the CSF space (often from ruptured aneurysm). Classic: sudden 'thunderclap' headache — worst of life.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: The classic patterns help you anticipate who is at risk: epidural = young trauma patient + lucid interval; subdural = elderly/falls/anticoagulated + subtle onset; SAH = sudden catastrophic headache. All three are surgical emergencies. Your role: recognize the pattern, monitor neuro status vigilantly, prepare for rapid escalation.

🦴 What is this?
SCI — Spinal Cord Injury

Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) is damage to the spinal cord — the main information highway between the brain and the body. Injury can be complete (total loss of sensation and motor function below the injury level) or incomplete (some function preserved). Location matters: cervical injuries (C1–C8) affect arms, trunk, legs + potentially breathing. Thoracic (T1–T12) affects trunk and legs. Lumbar (L1–L5) affects legs. Two dangerous early complications: Spinal shock (temporary loss of all function below injury — resolves in days/weeks) and Neurogenic shock (loss of sympathetic tone → hypotension + bradycardia — NO tachycardia, distinguishing it from other shock). Long-term complication at T6+: Autonomic Dysreflexia.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: Priority nursing for acute SCI: immobilize (never move without spinal precautions), maintain airway (cervical injuries may paralyze respiratory muscles), monitor for neurogenic shock (vasopressors + atropine, NOT aggressive fluids alone), log-roll for turns. Long-term: prevent pressure injuries (cannot feel pain), bowel/bladder program, autonomic dysreflexia education (full bladder = most common trigger → sit up + check Foley first).
🦴 Spinal Cord Injury — Level Determines Function
The higher the injury level, the more functions are lost. C-spine injuries (especially C1-C4) are the most life-threatening due to respiratory muscle involvement. Always immobilize and log-roll.

🧓 What is this?
Dementia & Delirium — Critical Differences

These two are often confused but are completely different conditions. Dementia is a progressive, irreversible syndrome of cognitive decline — memory, reasoning, language, judgment all slowly deteriorate over months to years. Most common cause: Alzheimer's disease (beta-amyloid plaques + neurofibrillary tangles). Cannot be reversed. Delirium is an acute, sudden-onset confusion with fluctuating consciousness, often with hallucinations. It ALWAYS has a reversible underlying cause (infection, medications, metabolic disturbance, pain, urinary retention). The key clinical distinction: Dementia = gradual + stable. Delirium = sudden onset + fluctuating. A demented patient CAN develop delirium on top of their dementia — called 'delirium superimposed on dementia' and it is very common in hospitalized elderly.

🩺 Why nurses need to know this: The most important nursing action: if a patient suddenly becomes MORE confused than their baseline → think delirium first. Find and fix the cause: check for UTI, pain, urinary retention, hypoxia, medication review, electrolytes. Delirium is preventable and treatable — it is NOT just 'normal for old people.' DELIRIUM INCREASES MORTALITY. Non-pharmacologic management first: reorientation, restore sleep/wake cycle, mobilization, familiar faces, minimize lines/catheters.

🏥 Module 2.2 — Clinical Snapshot: Trauma & ICP Floor Ready

These are the highest-yield clinical scenarios for your exam AND your neuro clinical placement.

📈 Increased ICP
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Monroe-Kellie: skull = rigid fixed box containing brain (80%), blood (10%), CSF (10%). When any component increases — bleed, oedema, tumour, excess CSF — the others must compensate. When compensation fails, ICP rises. High ICP compresses brain tissue → ↓CPP → brain ischaemia → herniation → death. CPP = MAP - ICP (goal >60 mmHg).
👁 What would I see?
  • Early: headache, nausea, vomiting (projectile, often WITHOUT nausea), decreased LOC, restlessness
  • Pupil changes: sluggish then fixed/dilated (unilateral = uncal herniation)
  • Late — Cushing's Triad (EMERGENCY):
    • ↑ Blood pressure (widened pulse pressure)
    • ↓ Heart rate (bradycardia)
    • Irregular/slow respirations
  • Posturing: decorticate (arms flexed) or decerebrate (arms extended = worse)
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • HOB 30° (promotes venous drainage) — exception: SCI/unstable spine = flat
  • Neuro checks (GCS, pupils, vital signs) every 1-2 hours
  • Minimise stimulation — cluster care, limit suctioning, quiet room
  • Maintain normothermia (fever ↑ metabolic demand → ↑ICP)
  • Administer mannitol or hypertonic saline as ordered
  • Avoid anything that raises ICP: coughing, straining, Valsalva
  • Stool softeners to prevent Valsalva
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Cushing's triad = early ICP sign" — No. It is a LATE and ominous sign — herniation is occurring
  • "Vomiting in ↑ICP = patient needs antiemetics" — Projectile vomiting is a sign of ↑ICP, not nausea. Address ICP
  • "Sedate the patient to keep them calm" — Sedation can mask deteriorating neuro status
  • "HOB flat improves cerebral blood flow" — True for ischemia, but NOT safe with ↑ICP; 30° is the standard
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Cushing's triad — call provider immediately
  • GCS drop of 2+ points
  • New pupillary changes (asymmetry, sluggish, non-reactive)
  • New posturing
  • Seizure activity
  • Any sudden change in vital signs in context of head injury
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
Frequent mini-neuro checks: "Can you tell me your name? Squeeze my fingers. Show me your teeth." A patient who was answering questions and suddenly stops = report immediately. The window between 'needs attention' and 'herniation' in ↑ICP is very narrow.
🤕 Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Primary injury: direct mechanical damage at moment of impact (contusion, laceration, DAI, skull fracture, haematoma). Secondary injury: subsequent cascade — swelling, hypoxia, hypotension, inflammation — extends damage beyond the original site. Nursing goal: prevent secondary injury because primary injury is done.
👁 What would I see?
  • GCS scale: Eyes (1-4) + Verbal (1-5) + Motor (1-6) = 3–15. Mild TBI ≥13, Moderate 9-12, Severe ≤8
  • Epidural haematoma hallmark: brief LOC → LUCID INTERVAL (seems fine) → rapid deterioration — DO NOT be fooled
  • Raccoon eyes / Battle's sign = basilar skull fracture (blood tracking into periorbital tissues or behind ear)
  • CSF rhinorrhoea or otorrhoea (clear fluid from nose/ear = halo sign on gauze)
  • Diffuse axonal injury: immediate deep coma, no lucid interval
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • Prevent secondary injury: maintain O2 sat >95%, SBP >90, normoglycaemia (70-140), normothermia
  • GCS every 1-2 hours — document trends
  • Do NOT give sedation that masks neuro status without MD order
  • ICP monitoring protocol if ordered
  • Do NOT give aspirin, NSAIDs (bleeding risk)
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Lucid interval = patient is fine, can rest" — NEVER. Lucid interval = arterial epidural bleed = surgical emergency. Watch every minute
  • "Battle's sign/raccoon eyes appear immediately" — They appear 12-24h after injury, not immediately
  • "GCS 13 = not serious" — Mild TBI still requires monitoring; can deteriorate
  • Clear fluid from ear/nose is CSF until proven otherwise — do NOT pack or block it (can increase ICP)
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Any GCS drop ≥2 points from baseline
  • Worsening or new headache after initial improvement
  • Lucid interval in a trauma patient — call immediately
  • Pupil changes, posturing, Cushing's triad
  • CSF leak (halo sign or confirmed clear fluid)
  • Seizures
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
Halo test for CSF leak: place drop of fluid from nose/ear on gauze. CSF = halo ring (lighter fluid migrates outward from blood). Positive halo = basilar skull fracture. Do NOT pack the nose or ear — this increases infection risk and ICP. Elevate HOB and keep patient still.
🦴 Spinal Cord Injury + Neurogenic Shock
▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
SCI: trauma severs or compresses spinal cord → permanent loss of motor/sensory function below injury level. Neurogenic shock (T6 or above): sudden loss of sympathetic nervous system input → uncontrolled vasodilation → ↓BP AND bradycardia (no compensatory tachycardia). Spinal shock (neurological): all cord function temporarily absent below injury, regardless of level — reflexes return when it resolves.
👁 What would I see?
  • Neurogenic shock: hypotension + bradycardia + warm, dry skin below injury + no sweating = "warm shock"
  • Spinal shock: flaccid paralysis, absent reflexes, loss of sensation — temporary, resolves days to weeks later
  • Priapism (persistent erection) = sign of SCI-related loss of sympathetic control
  • Level of injury determines what functions are lost: C3-C5 = diaphragm affected ("C3, 4, 5 keep the diaphragm alive")
⚡ What is my #1 nursing priority?
  • Spinal immobilisation first — do NOT move without full spinal precautions
  • Airway — cervical injuries may require immediate intubation (diaphragm paralysis)
  • For neurogenic shock: vasopressors (norepinephrine) + IV fluids + atropine for bradycardia
  • Foley catheter (neurogenic bladder — retention is universal)
  • Bowel programme (neurogenic bowel)
  • Pressure injury prevention (no sensation = cannot feel pressure)
🪤 What is the NCLEX/test trap?
  • "Neurogenic shock = give lots of fluids" — Fluids alone are insufficient; vasopressors needed to restore tone
  • "Neurogenic shock = tachycardia" — No. ABSENCE of tachycardia distinguishes neurogenic from hypovolemic shock
  • "Spinal shock = permanent" — No, spinal shock is temporary. The return of reflexes signals its resolution
  • "All SCI patients have complete cord injury" — Incomplete injuries (central cord, Brown-Séquard) can have partial function
📞 What would I report to the provider?
  • Autonomic dysreflexia: sudden SBP spike, pounding headache, bradycardia, flushing above lesion = EMERGENCY. Sit up, check Foley, check bowel, call MD. Can cause stroke if untreated
  • Respiratory rate changes (cervical injury = diaphragm at risk)
  • New skin breakdown (patient cannot feel or report it)
  • Urinary retention signs (full bladder is the #1 trigger for autonomic dysreflexia)
📌 Clinical pearl / practical tip
Remember: in a T6+ SCI patient, a full bladder = potential life-threatening autonomic dysreflexia. Checking the Foley for patency is not a small task — it is a safety-critical nursing action. On clinical: if your SCI patient suddenly complains of a pounding headache, sit them up and check the Foley before anything else.

rt-PA / Alteplase (tPA) — "The Clot Buster"
Ischemic Stroke
▾
Drug Class
Thrombolytic (fibrinolytic)
How It Works
Activates plasminogen → converts to plasmin → breaks down fibrin in the clot → dissolves the blockage and restores blood flow to the ischemic penumbra. Think of it as "chemical scissors" cutting up the clot.
Time Window
Within 4 hours of symptom onset (most effective within 90 min). If onset unknown, cannot give — use last-known-well time.
Before Giving
CT without contrast FIRST to rule out hemorrhage. BP must be <185/110 mmHg. Confirm ischemic (not hemorrhagic) stroke.
Side Effects
Bleeding (anywhere — gums, IV sites, GI), intracranial hemorrhage (most serious), angioedema
Contraindications
Hemorrhagic stroke, active internal bleeding, recent surgery (<3 months), anticoagulation, severe HTN, blood glucose <50 or >400
Nursing Watch
After giving: sudden severe headache, ↓ LOC, new neurological deficits = STOP infusion, call MD — may signal intracranial bleed. No IM injections, no arterial sticks for 24h. Monitor BP q15min.
⛔ ABSOLUTE RULE: Never give tPA for hemorrhagic stroke. It will turn a bleed into a catastrophic hemorrhage.
NCLEX TIPFirst step in acute stroke = CT without contrast (rule out hemorrhage). tPA = ischemic only, within 4h, BP controlled.
Mannitol (Osmitrol) — "Brain Dehydrator"
↑ICP / Cerebral Edema
▾
Drug Class
Osmotic diuretic
How It Works
Creates an osmotic gradient between blood and brain tissue — pulls water OUT of swollen brain cells and into the bloodstream → reduces cerebral edema and lowers ICP. Think of it as a sponge drawing water away from the brain.
Route / Form
IV infusion — use a filter (crystalizes). Give via dedicated line.
Monitor
Urine output (causes massive diuresis), electrolytes (Na, K), serum osmolality (keep <320 mOsm/kg), ICP readings, BP
Side Effects
Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance (hyponatremia, hypokalemia), hypotension, rebound ICP rise if stopped too abruptly
Contraindications
Anuria (no urine output), severe dehydration, active intracranial bleeding (relative)
✅ Nursing: Foley catheter is usually inserted before giving mannitol — expect large volumes of urine output rapidly.
NCLEX TIPMannitol = osmotic diuretic for ↑ICP. Monitor urine output and electrolytes. Give through a filter. Not a cure — temporary measure while addressing the cause.
Nimodipine (Nimotop) — "Vasospasm Preventer"
SAH / Vasospasm
▾
Drug Class
Calcium channel blocker (dihydropyridine, cerebral-selective)
How It Works
Blocks calcium entry into smooth muscle of cerebral arteries → prevents vasospasm (dangerous narrowing of vessels) after subarachnoid hemorrhage. Blood from SAH irritates vessels; nimodipine keeps them relaxed.
Timing
Started within 96 hours of SAH onset, continued for 21 days. Peak vasospasm risk = days 4–14.
Route
PO or NG tube ONLY — never IV (causes fatal hypotension). Oral capsule contents may be withdrawn via syringe if patient cannot swallow.
Side Effects
Hypotension (most common), headache, flushing, edema
Nursing Watch
BP before each dose — hold if SBP <90. Never give IV.
⛔ NEVER give nimodipine IV — can cause severe hypotension and cardiac arrest. Always PO/NG only.
NCLEX TIPNimodipine = SAH vasospasm prevention. Give PO/NG only, never IV. Monitor BP. Start within 96h, run for 21 days.
Levodopa + Carbidopa (Sinemet) — "Dopamine Replacement"
Parkinson's Disease
▾
Drug Class
Dopamine precursor + peripheral decarboxylase inhibitor
How It Works
Parkinson's = dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra die off. Levodopa crosses the blood-brain barrier and converts to dopamine in the brain. Carbidopa blocks peripheral conversion (in the body before it reaches the brain) so more L-dopa gets to the brain and peripheral side effects are reduced. Carbidopa = the bodyguard that gets L-dopa to the brain safely.
Side Effects
Dyskinesia (involuntary movements — sign of too much), "on-off" fluctuations (drug wears off between doses), nausea, orthostatic hypotension, hallucinations, impulse control issues
Timing Matters
Give on a strict schedule — missing doses causes severe symptom return. Hospital nurses must administer on time. Do not hold without MD order.
Food Interactions
High-protein meals reduce absorption (amino acids compete for transport). Take 30–60 min before meals.
Contraindications
MAO inhibitors (hypertensive crisis risk), narrow-angle glaucoma
✅ Nursing: Parkinson's meds must be given on time in the hospital — a late dose can leave a patient unable to move, speak, or swallow safely.
NCLEX TIPCarbidopa prevents peripheral L-dopa breakdown so more reaches the brain. Watch for dyskinesias (too much dopamine effect). Never abruptly stop — can cause neuroleptic malignant-like syndrome.
Donepezil (Aricept) + Rivastigmine / Galantamine
Alzheimer's / Dementia
▾
Drug Class
Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibitor
How It Works
Alzheimer's destroys acetylcholine-producing neurons. AChE inhibitors block the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine → more ACh remains in synapses → temporarily improves memory and cognition. Think of it as preventing the cleanup crew from removing the neurotransmitter too fast.
Realistic Expectations
Slows decline — does NOT reverse or cure Alzheimer's. Family teaching is crucial: the goal is to maintain function longer, not restore it.
Side Effects
GI: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (most common — cholinergic stimulation of GI tract). Also bradycardia, insomnia, muscle cramps. Give at bedtime to reduce GI effects.
Donepezil vs Memantine
Donepezil = mild-moderate AD. Memantine (Namenda, NMDA antagonist) = moderate-severe AD. Often used together in later stages.
NCLEX TIPAChE inhibitors slow decline but do NOT cure dementia. Common side effects = GI (nausea, diarrhea). Give donepezil at bedtime. Memantine = added for moderate-severe disease.
Phenytoin (Dilantin) + Levetiracetam (Keppra)
Seizures / Epilepsy
▾
Drug Class
Phenytoin: Sodium channel blocker. Levetiracetam: Modifies SV2A protein (synaptic vesicle release).
How It Works
Phenytoin stabilizes neuron membranes by blocking sodium channels → prevents the rapid, repetitive firing that causes seizures. Think of it as putting speed bumps on the neuron highway.
Phenytoin Range
Narrow therapeutic range: 10–20 mcg/mL. Below = seizures. Above = toxicity.
Phenytoin Toxicity
SAND: Sedation, Ataxia (unsteady gait), Nystagmus (involuntary eye movement), Diplopia (double vision)
Phenytoin Side Effects
Gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth — teach oral hygiene), hirsutism, rash, teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy)
IV Phenytoin
Must be in NS only (precipitates in D5W). Max rate 50 mg/min — faster causes cardiac arrhythmias. Monitor ECG and BP during infusion.
Keppra (Levetiracetam)
Fewer drug interactions, no need for level monitoring. Common side effect: mood changes/irritability. Safer in pregnancy than phenytoin.
Key Teaching
Never skip doses. Do not stop abruptly (lowers seizure threshold). Avoid alcohol. Wear medical alert ID.
⛔ Phenytoin IV: NS only, slow rate. Give via filter. Extravasation = tissue necrosis (purple glove syndrome).
NCLEX TIPPhenytoin: therapeutic level 10-20. Toxicity = SAND (Sedation, Ataxia, Nystagmus, Diplopia). Gingival hyperplasia = teach oral hygiene. IV in NS only, not D5W.
Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) + Edrophonium (Tensilon)
Myasthenia Gravis
▾
Drug Class
Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibitor — but at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), not the brain
How It Works
MG = autoimmune destruction of ACh receptors at NMJ → muscles don't respond. Pyridostigmine prevents ACh breakdown → more ACh available to stimulate remaining receptors → temporary muscle strength improvement. It's making the most of what little receptor space is left.
Timing Is Critical
Must give exactly on schedule. Even 30 min late = patient may lose ability to chew, swallow, or breathe.
Side Effects
Cholinergic (SLUDGE): Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation/Diarrhea, GI cramps, Emesis. Also bradycardia, bronchospasm.
Myasthenic vs Cholinergic Crisis
Myasthenic crisis = undertreated, not enough drug → muscle weakness → give more Mestinon, consider IVIG/plasmapheresis. Cholinergic crisis = too much drug → SLUDGE signs + weakness → HOLD drug, give atropine. Tensilon test differentiates: if it improves weakness = myasthenic; if worsens = cholinergic.
Edrophonium
Short-acting AChE inhibitor. Used for Tensilon test to diagnose MG or differentiate crisis types. Have atropine at bedside.
⛔ Most critical nursing action in MG: assess respiratory status before and after doses. Myasthenic crisis can cause respiratory failure requiring intubation.
NCLEX TIPPyridostigmine = AChE inhibitor for MG. Give on strict schedule — late dose = respiratory risk. Distinguish myasthenic crisis (more drug) vs cholinergic crisis (hold drug + atropine). Tensilon test differentiates.
IVIG + Plasmapheresis (Plasma Exchange)
GBS / MG Crisis
▾
IVIG
Intravenous immunoglobulin. Gives large doses of donor antibodies → overwhelms and neutralizes the patient's abnormal autoantibodies. Mechanism: saturates Fc receptors, blocks autoantibody binding. Think of it as flooding the scene with friendly soldiers so the enemy antibodies can't attack.
Plasmapheresis
Filters blood through a machine that removes plasma (which contains the harmful autoantibodies) and returns red blood cells with donor plasma or albumin. Like doing a full oil change — takes the bad stuff out directly.
Which is Used When
Both are used for GBS and myasthenic crisis. IVIG is easier to administer. Plasmapheresis works faster for acute crisis but requires large-bore IV access or central line.
GBS Use
GBS = autoimmune demyelination of peripheral nerves. IVIG or plasmapheresis within 2 weeks of symptom onset reduces severity and speeds recovery. Monitor respirations — GBS can ascend to diaphragm.
Nursing
IVIG: infusion reactions (fever, chills, headache — slow rate); monitor for anaphylaxis in IgA-deficient patients. Plasmapheresis: fluid shifts, hypocalcemia, clotting issues; monitor VS closely.
NCLEX TIPIVIG = adds good antibodies. Plasmapheresis = removes bad antibodies. Both used for GBS and MG crisis. GBS priority nursing = monitor respiratory rate and depth — ascending paralysis can stop breathing.
Sumatriptan (Imitrex) + Ergotamines
Migraine / Cluster HA
▾
Drug Class
Triptan (serotonin 5-HT1B/1D agonist)
How It Works
Binds to serotonin receptors on cranial blood vessels → vasoconstriction → reduces the vasodilation and inflammation that drives migraine pain. Also blocks pain signal transmission in trigeminal nerve pathway.
Route Options
PO, SQ injection, nasal spray. SQ fastest onset (10–15 min). PO slowest but longest lasting.
Contraindications
Coronary artery disease, uncontrolled HTN, hemiplegic migraine, history of stroke or TIA, MAO inhibitors (serotonin syndrome risk)
Side Effects
Flushing, tingling, chest tightness/heaviness (usually benign, but rule out cardiac), dizziness, rebound headache with overuse
Ergotamines
Older agents; more side effects (nausea, vasospasm); contraindicated in pregnancy; not first-line anymore but still used for refractory cases.
NCLEX TIPTriptans contraindicated in CAD, stroke history, uncontrolled HTN. Chest tightness is a common (usually benign) side effect but assess for cardiac cause first. Avoid in hemiplegic migraine.
Baclofen (Lioresal) — "Spasticity Relaxer"
MS / SCI Spasticity
▾
Drug Class
GABA-B receptor agonist (centrally-acting muscle relaxant)
How It Works
Activates GABA-B receptors in the spinal cord → inhibits motor neurons → reduces spasticity (involuntary muscle stiffness). Does not affect voluntary motor strength.
Routes
Oral (systemic) or intrathecal pump (directly into CSF for severe spasticity — smaller dose needed, fewer side effects)
Side Effects
Drowsiness, dizziness, weakness, confusion (especially in elderly). Respiratory depression at high doses.
CRITICAL

Intrathecal Pump

⛔ Never abruptly stop baclofen — severe withdrawal can be fatal. Taper slowly. Educate patient and family.
NCLEX TIPBaclofen = GABA agonist for spasticity. Never stop abruptly (seizures, hyperthermia = life-threatening withdrawal). Causes sedation — fall precautions.

🫀 Module 2.0 — Stroke Medications

rt-PA / Alteplase (tPA)
Ischemic Stroke▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Activates plasminogen → plasmin → breaks down fibrin in the clot → dissolves the blockage and restores blood flow to the penumbra. Think of it as chemical scissors cutting apart the clot from inside the blood vessel.
👁 What would I see?
  • If working: neuro deficits improve (arm strength returns, speech clears)
  • If side effect: bleeding anywhere — IV site ooze, gum bleeding, hematuria, GI bleed
  • Worst: sudden severe headache + ↓LOC after giving = intracranial hemorrhage
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • CT without contrast FIRST — confirm ischemic, no hemorrhage
  • BP must be <185/110 before giving — control with labetalol/nicardipine if needed
  • Check blood glucose — must be 50–400
  • During infusion: neuro checks every 15 min, BP q15 min x 2h, then q30 min x 6h
  • No IM injections, no arterial lines, no Foley insertion for 30 min post-tPA
🪤 The test trap
  • "Give tPA, then do the CT" — NEVER. CT comes first, always
  • "tPA works up to 4.5 hours" — Standard eligibility is 4 HOURS
  • "tPA for hemorrhagic stroke" — ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED. Fatal.
📞 What to report
  • Sudden severe headache during/after infusion = stop infusion, call MD immediately
  • Decreased LOC or new neuro deficits after tPA started
  • BP above 180/105 during infusion
  • Any active bleeding (apply pressure to puncture sites)
Nimodipine (Nimotop)
SAH Vasospasm▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Calcium channel blocker selective for cerebral arteries. After subarachnoid hemorrhage, blood in the subarachnoid space irritates vessel walls and triggers vasospasm (dangerous narrowing). Nimodipine relaxes the smooth muscle of cerebral vessels to prevent this. Vasospasm peaks days 4-14 post-SAH.
👁 What would I see?
  • If working: preventing the delayed neuro deterioration from vasospasm (days 4-14)
  • Side effects: hypotension (most common), flushing, headache, nausea
  • Signs of vasospasm NOT prevented: new focal deficits, ↓ LOC in SAH patient days 4-14
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • GIVE PO OR NG TUBE ONLY — NEVER IV
  • Check BP before every dose — hold if SBP <90
  • Give on strict schedule every 4h for 21 days from SAH onset
  • If patient cannot swallow capsule: withdraw contents with syringe, give via NG tube
🪤 The test trap
  • "Give nimodipine IV for faster effect" — IV nimodipine causes severe hypotension and cardiac arrest. PO/NG only.
  • "Stop nimodipine after vasospasm risk passes" — Give full 21-day course regardless
📞 What to report
  • SBP <90 (hold dose and call MD)
  • New neuro deficits in SAH patient (vasospasm occurring despite medication)
  • Arrhythmias (rare but possible)

💊 Module 2.1 — Neurodegenerative Medications

Phenytoin (Dilantin) / Levetiracetam (Keppra)
Seizures▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Phenytoin: blocks voltage-gated sodium channels → stabilises neuron membranes → prevents the rapid repetitive firing that causes seizures. Think of it as speed bumps on the neuron highway. Keppra (levetiracetam): modifies synaptic vesicle release — different mechanism, fewer drug interactions.
👁 What would I see?
  • Phenytoin therapeutic: patient is seizure-free with level 10-20 mcg/mL
  • Phenytoin toxicity (SAND): Sedation, Ataxia (unsteady), Nystagmus (eye bouncing), Diplopia (double vision)
  • Long-term: gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth), hirsutism, rash
  • Keppra side effects: mood changes, irritability ("Kepprage")
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • Check phenytoin level before and after dose adjustments
  • IV phenytoin: NS ONLY (not D5W — precipitates), max rate 50 mg/min, monitor ECG and BP during infusion
  • Never stop anticonvulsants abruptly — lowers seizure threshold → breakthrough seizures
  • Teach: take on time, no alcohol, wear medical alert ID
🪤 The test trap
  • "Phenytoin can be mixed in D5W" — NEVER. It precipitates immediately in dextrose
  • "Therapeutic level is 20-30 mcg/mL" — No, it is 10-20. Above 20 = toxicity
  • "Phenytoin IV can be pushed fast" — No, max 50 mg/min. Too fast = cardiac arrhythmia, hypotension
  • "Purple glove syndrome" — IV phenytoin extravasation causes tissue necrosis
📞 What to report
  • SAND signs (sedation, ataxia, nystagmus, diplopia) = toxicity → check level
  • Breakthrough seizures despite therapeutic levels
  • Rash (Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk)
  • Cardiac changes during IV administration
Levodopa + Carbidopa (Sinemet)
Parkinson's▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Parkinson's = dopamine deficiency in the substantia nigra. Dopamine itself cannot cross the blood-brain barrier. Levodopa (L-dopa) CAN cross and converts to dopamine in the brain. Carbidopa is added to block peripheral conversion of L-dopa before it reaches the brain — so more L-dopa gets to where it is needed and peripheral side effects (nausea, vomiting) are reduced.
👁 What would I see?
  • If working: tremor reduces, movement improves, speech clearer
  • "On" time = medication working well; "Off" time = wearing off, stiffness returns
  • Dyskinesias = involuntary writhing movements = TOO MUCH dopamine effect (over-treated)
  • Side effects: orthostatic hypotension, nausea, hallucinations, impulse control issues
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • Give EXACTLY on schedule — set a timer, never hold without MD order
  • Give 30-60 min BEFORE meals (high-protein meals compete for absorption)
  • Assess swallowing before each dose and meal
  • Fall precautions (orthostatic hypotension, balance impairment)
🪤 The test trap
  • "Hold Sinemet to reduce fall risk" — Holding worsens rigidity and increases fall risk more
  • "Carbidopa is the active ingredient" — No, levodopa is. Carbidopa just protects it in transit
  • "Give with protein-rich food" — Protein reduces absorption; give before or with low-protein food
📞 What to report
  • Inability to swallow (medication and food aspiration risk)
  • Dyskinesias (involuntary movements — too much L-dopa)
  • Sudden inability to move after dose (wearing off — timing adjustment needed)
  • Hallucinations or confusion (dopaminergic side effect)
Pyridostigmine (Mestinon)
Myasthenia Gravis▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
MG = autoimmune destruction of ACh receptors at the NMJ. Pyridostigmine is an acetylcholinesterase (AChE) inhibitor — it blocks the enzyme that breaks down ACh. More ACh stays in the NMJ → stimulates remaining receptors longer → temporary muscle strength improvement. It does NOT fix the underlying immune problem — just manages symptoms.
👁 What would I see?
  • If working: ptosis improves, grip stronger, speech clearer, swallowing easier
  • Cholinergic side effects (too much ACh everywhere): SLUDGE — Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation/Diarrhoea, GI cramps, Emesis + bradycardia, bronchospasm, muscle fasciculations
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • Assess respirations (rate, depth, O2 sat) BEFORE every dose
  • Give exactly on schedule — even 30 min late = patient may not be able to swallow or breathe safely
  • Assess swallowing before meals
  • Keep atropine at bedside (reverses cholinergic effects)
  • Know the difference between myasthenic and cholinergic crisis
🪤 The test trap
  • "Both crises look like weakness, so just give more Mestinon" — WRONG and potentially fatal. Cholinergic crisis = HOLD drug + give atropine. Use Tensilon test to distinguish.
  • "SLUDGE = allergic reaction" — No. SLUDGE = classic cholinergic toxicity from AChE inhibition
📞 What to report
  • Any respiratory change (both crises can lead to respiratory arrest)
  • SLUDGE signs after a dose = cholinergic crisis → HOLD drug, call MD
  • Worsening weakness that does not improve after dose
  • Fever or infection (common MG crisis trigger)
Donepezil (Aricept) / Memantine (Namenda)
Alzheimer's▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Alzheimer's destroys ACh-producing neurons. Donepezil is an AChE inhibitor — prevents breakdown of remaining ACh in brain synapses → temporarily improves memory and cognition. Memantine blocks NMDA receptors — prevents excess glutamate stimulation that damages neurons. Used together in moderate-severe AD. Neither drug reverses or cures the disease.
👁 What would I see?
  • If working: slower rate of decline (not improvement), better daily function maintenance
  • Donepezil side effects: GI (nausea, diarrhoea, vomiting — most common), bradycardia, insomnia
  • Memantine side effects: dizziness, confusion, constipation
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • Give donepezil at bedtime (reduces GI side effects, insomnia less noticeable)
  • Educate family: the goal is slowing decline, NOT restoring lost function
  • Monitor for GI side effects especially when starting or increasing dose
  • Monitor heart rate (donepezil can cause bradycardia)
🪤 The test trap
  • "Donepezil will improve memory" — It slows decline, does NOT restore lost function
  • "AChE inhibitors used in MG = same as in AD" — Both are AChE inhibitors but completely different drugs for different locations (pyridostigmine = NMJ; donepezil = brain)
📞 What to report
  • Persistent GI side effects (nausea, diarrhoea) — may need dose adjustment
  • Bradycardia or syncope (donepezil)
  • Sudden worsening of cognitive function (new delirium on top of dementia?)

🤕 Module 2.2 — Trauma/ICP Medications

Mannitol (Osmitrol)
↑ICP / Cerebral Oedema▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
Osmotic diuretic. Creates a high-concentration gradient in the bloodstream that pulls water OUT of swollen brain cells and into the blood vessels → fluid leaves the brain → cerebral oedema and ICP decrease. Think of it as a dehydrating sponge drawing fluid away from the brain.
👁 What would I see?
  • If working: ICP monitor reading decreases, patient becomes more alert
  • Effects: massive urine output (diuresis) within 30-60 min of infusion
  • Side effects: dehydration, electrolyte imbalances (↓Na, ↓K), hypotension if fluid not replaced
  • Rebound ICP rise if stopped abruptly after prolonged use
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • Insert Foley catheter before giving (expect high urine volumes)
  • Give through an in-line filter (mannitol crystallises)
  • Monitor serum osmolality (keep <320 mOsm/kg — above this = toxicity risk)
  • Monitor electrolytes (Na, K) and urine output
  • Monitor BP — osmotic diuresis can cause hypotension
🪤 The test trap
  • "Mannitol lowers BP, use it for hypertension" — No. Mannitol lowers ICP specifically; BP effects are a side effect
  • "Give mannitol without a filter" — Crystals can form; always use a filter
  • "Mannitol is a cure for ↑ICP" — It is a temporary bridge while the underlying cause is treated
📞 What to report
  • Urine output <30 mL/hr (mannitol should be producing diuresis)
  • Signs of dehydration or hypotension
  • Serum osmolality approaching 320 mOsm/kg
  • Signs of worsening ICP despite mannitol (Cushing's triad)
  • Electrolyte abnormalities
IVIG + Plasmapheresis
GBS / MG Crisis▾
🫀 What is happening in the body?
IVIG: infuses large amounts of donor antibodies (immunoglobulins) → overwhelms and neutralises the patient's own pathological autoantibodies → reduces immune attack on nerve myelin (GBS) or NMJ receptors (MG). Plasmapheresis: blood is processed through a machine that physically removes the plasma (which contains the harmful autoantibodies) and replaces it with donor plasma or albumin — directly removes the attacking agents.
👁 What would I see?
  • IVIG: infusion reactions common — fever, chills, headache, flushing during the infusion (slow the rate)
  • Rare but serious: anaphylaxis (IgA-deficient patients)
  • Plasmapheresis: fluid shifts, hypocalcaemia (tingling, muscle cramps), clotting changes, hypotension
  • Both: improvement in muscle strength and/or reduced disease progression over days
⚡ Nurse's #1 priority
  • IVIG: start slowly, monitor vital signs throughout infusion, have diphenhydramine and epinephrine available
  • Plasmapheresis: large-bore IV or central line required; monitor VS closely; replace calcium if tingling develops
  • Both: respiratory monitoring is critical — GBS and MG crises both risk respiratory failure
🪤 The test trap
  • "IVIG and plasmapheresis do the same thing the same way" — No. IVIG adds good antibodies; plasmapheresis removes bad ones. Both treat the effect, different mechanisms
  • "Start plasmapheresis through a peripheral IV" — Requires large-bore access or central line due to flow rate
📞 What to report
  • Respiratory changes during or after treatment (GBS/MG patients are fragile)
  • Infusion reactions during IVIG (slow rate, pre-medicate if needed)
  • Signs of hypocalcaemia during plasmapheresis (tingling, Chvostek's sign)
  • Hypotension or haemodynamic instability

💊 Quick Disease Reference — One Line Each

Module 2.1 — Neurodegenerative

  • Parkinson's: dopamine↓, substantia nigra, resting tremor, Sinemet (give on time!)
  • Alzheimer's: plaques + tangles, progressive, donepezil slows (does not cure)
  • MS: CNS demyelination, relapsing-remitting, heat worsens (Uhthoff's), Lhermitte's sign
  • GBS: PNS demyelination, ascending paralysis, areflexia, IVIG/plasmapheresis, watch breathing
  • MG: ACh receptor destruction, weakness worse with use, Mestinon, know both crises
  • ALS: UMN+LMN degeneration, no cure, ventilator support, G-tube, progressive
  • Huntington's: CAG repeat, autosomal dominant, chorea, psychiatric changes, cognitive decline
  • Seizures: electrical storm, safety during, time it, NEVER put in mouth, postictal normal

Module 2.2 — Trauma & ICP

  • ↑ICP: Monroe-Kellie, HOB 30°, mannitol, Cushing's = LATE = emergency, CPP >60
  • Epidural: arterial, lucid interval, then rapid drop = surgical emergency
  • Subdural: venous, slower, elderly/anticoag at risk, acute vs chronic
  • SAH: thunderclap headache, aneurysm rupture, nimodipine x21 days, vasospasm d4-14
  • TBI: prevent secondary injury, GCS trends, no sedation masking neuro status
  • Neurogenic shock: T6+, ↓BP + bradycardia + warm dry skin, vasopressors + fluids
  • Spinal shock: temporary cord silence, areflexia, resolves — NOT the same as neurogenic
  • Autonomic dysreflexia: T6+, noxious stimulus → HTN crisis, sit up + check Foley FIRST
  • Delirium vs dementia: acute vs gradual, reversible vs progressive, new confusion = delirium first

❌ "Give tPA immediately" → ✅ CT first, THEN tPA if ischemic
❌ "Left stroke = left weakness" → ✅ CONTRALATERAL — left stroke = right weakness
❌ "Nimodipine can be given IV" → ✅ PO/NG tube ONLY. IV = fatal hypotension
❌ "Lucid interval = patient is fine" → ✅ Lucid interval = epidural hematoma = surgical emergency

🚨 Priority Nursing Actions

Use the ABCs + clinical priority framework. Actions listed in order of nursing priority.

STROKE — ISCHEMIC
  1. Establish exact last-known-well time — determines tPA eligibility (≤4.5 hr window)
  2. Maintain airway, monitor O₂ sat — keep ≥94%; hypoxia worsens ischemia
  3. Do NOT lower BP unless >220/120 — collateral flow depends on high BP
  4. Obtain IV access × 2; draw labs — glucose, coags, CBC before tPA
  5. Perform NIHSS assessment — baseline neurological scoring
  6. Keep patient NPO — aspiration risk; dysphagia common with stroke
  7. Prepare for CT head stat — rule out hemorrhage before any treatment
⏱️ Time-sensitive: "Time is brain" — 1.9 million neurons die every minute of delay
STROKE — HEMORRHAGIC
  1. Elevate HOB 30° — reduces ICP; do NOT lay flat
  2. Control BP per orders — target SBP <140 to limit hematoma expansion
  3. Reverse anticoagulation immediately — Vitamin K + FFP for warfarin; protamine for heparin
  4. Monitor LOC q1h with GCS — declining GCS = expanding bleed
  5. Prevent Valsalva — no straining, stool softeners, limit coughing
  6. Seizure precautions — padded rails, suction at bedside
  7. Prepare for neurosurgical consult / OR — large bleeds may need evacuation
🚫 Never give tPA for hemorrhagic stroke — it will make the bleed worse
↑ ICP / HERNIATION RISK
  1. HOB 30–45°, midline head — promote venous drainage; avoid neck flexion/rotation
  2. Maintain MAP 70–90 mmHg — ensure cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP − ICP)
  3. Restrict fluids + administer Mannitol — osmotic diuresis to reduce cerebral edema
  4. Control pain and agitation — stimulation increases ICP
  5. Avoid hyperthermia — fever dramatically worsens ICP; antipyretics + cooling
  6. Monitor pupils q1h — fixed, dilated pupil = herniation emergency
  7. Avoid clustering care activities — space interventions to allow ICP recovery
☠️ Cushing's Triad = herniation: Bradycardia + Hypertension + Irregular respirations → call MD STAT
SEIZURE
  1. Protect from injury — do NOT restrain — clear area, pad rails, nothing in mouth
  2. Time the seizure — >5 min = status epilepticus emergency
  3. Turn to lateral position — recovery position prevents aspiration
  4. Maintain airway; suction if needed — secretions pool during post-ictal phase
  5. Administer O₂ via mask — hypoxia occurs during seizure activity
  6. Obtain IV access for Lorazepam/Diazepam — benzodiazepine is first-line >5 min
  7. Monitor post-ictal state — Todd's paralysis, confusion expected; document duration
SPINAL CORD INJURY
  1. Immobilize spine immediately — C-collar + backboard; log-roll for all turns
  2. Airway priority for C1–C4 injuries — diaphragm paralysis; may need immediate intubation
  3. Watch for neurogenic shock — hypotension + bradycardia (no catecholamines below lesion)
  4. Avoid hypotension — MAP ≥85 to perfuse injured cord
  5. Monitor respiratory pattern q1h — ascending edema can compromise breathing
  6. Bladder management — insert Foley; neurogenic bladder is immediate
  7. Watch for autonomic dysreflexia (T6 and above) — pounding headache + hypertension = emergency
MYASTHENIC VS CHOLINERGIC CRISIS
  1. Assess respiratory status FIRST — NIF <−20 cmH₂O or VC <1L = intubate
  2. Differentiate the crisis type — Tensilon test; myasthenic improves, cholinergic worsens
  3. Hold pyridostigmine if cholinergic — SLUDGE symptoms: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI, Emesis
  4. Prepare for IVIG or Plasmapheresis — removes acetylcholine receptor antibodies
  5. Strict swallowing precautions — aspiration risk very high
🔑 Memory tip: Myasthenic = "More meds needed"; Cholinergic = "Too many meds, STOP"
GUILLAIN-BARRÉ SYNDROME
  1. Respiratory monitoring is #1 priority — weakness ascends; check respiratory rate, depth, O₂ sat every hour
  2. Serial pulmonary function tests — vital capacity <15 mL/kg = ventilatory support needed
  3. Autonomic monitoring — cardiac arrhythmias, BP swings; continuous telemetry
  4. Pain management — neuropathic pain is severe; opioids + gabapentin
  5. DVT prophylaxis — immobility + flaccid paralysis = high clot risk
  6. Nutritional support — NGT may be needed; swallowing assessment daily
PARKINSON'S DISEASE
  1. Administer Levodopa on schedule — never skip — abrupt discontinuation = Parkinson's hyperpyrexia syndrome (life-threatening)
  2. Fall prevention — festinating gait, postural instability; bed alarms, non-slip footwear
  3. Aspiration precautions — dysphagia common; upright positioning during meals, soft diet
  4. Constipation management — autonomic dysfunction; stool softeners, hydration, activity
  5. Orthostatic hypotension protocol — dangle before standing; compression stockings
  6. Skin integrity — seborrheic dermatitis common; frequent repositioning
